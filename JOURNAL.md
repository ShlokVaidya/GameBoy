## 1. Connecting the MCU to the LCD
The Waveshare 2.4-inch 65K TFT LCD is connected to the Teensy 4.1 using **SPI communication**, which is fast and uses fewer wires. Since both work on **3.3V**, the display’s **VCC** connects to **3.3V** on the Teensy and **GND** connects to **GND**.

For data transfer, **DIN** connects to **MOSI** and **CLK** connects to **SCK**. These pins send graphics data and timing signals from the Teensy to the display. **CS** connects to the Teensy **CS pin** to select the display during communication.

The **RST** pin is connected to **GPIO 8** so the Teensy can reset the screen when needed. The **BL** (backlight) pin is connected to **PWM pin 12**, which allows brightness control.

This setup gives smooth graphics, simple wiring, and good performance for games, menus, and UI on the DIY handheld console.

## 2. Connecting the MCU to the DAC
The Adafruit PCM5102 I2S DAC is connected to the Teensy 4.1 using the I2S audio interface for high-quality sound output. **VIN** of the DAC is connected to **5V VIN** on the Teensy, and **GND** is connected to **GND** to power the module.

For audio data signals, **LCK** (LRCLK / Word Select) is connected to **pin 20**, **DIN** is connected to **pin 7**, and **BCK** (Bit Clock) is connected to **pin 21** on the Teensy. The **SCK** pin is connected to **GND**, since it is not required in this setup.

This connection allows the Teensy 4.1 to send digital audio to the PCM5102, which converts it into clean analog sound output for speakers or amplifiers, making the console great for both music and game audio.

## 3. Connecting the Power Supply to the MCU
*Note:* The TP4056 module should be connected to the 5V/VIN pin of the Teensy 4.1, not the 3.3V pin. The Teensy uses its onboard regulator to safely manage the voltage. Connecting to 3.3V is not recommended, since it bypasses the board’s power regulation and may cause unstable operation.

# 4/19/2026 5 AM - Choosing a Prototype Board Over a Custom PCB

_Time spent: 0.75h_

During the planning stage of this project, I originally considered designing a custom PCB to make the console look cleaner and more professional. A custom PCB would help organize all the components neatly and reduce wiring. However, after researching the TFT screen I planned to use, I realized there was a major problem: the screen did not have clear or reliable dimensions available online, and there were no proper footprint files for it.

That meant designing a PCB would be risky. If the screen measurements were even slightly wrong, the mounting holes, pin positions, or connector placement could be off. One small mistake could make the PCB unusable, wasting both time and money. Since this is a DIY project where parts can vary a lot, I decided it was smarter not to take that risk right now.

Instead, I chose to use prototype boards. This is a much safer and more flexible option because I can place components where needed, test connections easily, and make changes during the build process. If I need to move wires, adjust layouts, or swap parts later, it is much easier on a prototype board than on a fixed PCB.

### Choosing the Components
I started this project by learning about the original Game Boy and why it became such a legendary handheld console. I watched videos about its history, hardware, gameplay, and design to understand how it worked with the limited technology of that time. I also checked teardown videos to see how everything was arranged inside, like the buttons, screen, speaker, and motherboard. This gave me a better idea of how Nintendo made something simple, portable, and fun that people still love today.

After that, I started thinking about how I could build my own version with a modern twist. I wanted it to have the classic retro feel of a Game Boy, but also be faster, smarter, and more useful. I researched different microcontrollers and compared their speed, memory, and features. After looking at multiple options, I chose the Teensy 4.1 because it is super powerful, compact, and great for projects that need displays, controls, and smooth performance. It felt like the perfect brain for my console.

I also wanted this device to be more than just for gaming, so I decided to add music features too. I looked into audio modules and found the Adafruit PCM5102 I2S DAC, which can give much better sound quality than a basic speaker setup. With that, the console could play music clearly while also handling game sound effects, making it way more fun and useful.
